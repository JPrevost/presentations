I work at Northwestern University's Galter Health Sciences Library.

Your Front Page Content is great right?

I don't thinks ours sucks more than most, but a lot of it is not actually used.

July 2nd, I pushed some code that started tracking all interaction with the front page of the site. Both categories of clicks and individual links clicked were tracked.

Category Data

  • Popular Links: 58.83%
  • Account: 28.05%
  • Top Nav: 6.48%
  • Global Header: 3.13%
  • Popular Drop Down: 1.00%
  • News Promos: 0.81%
  • Mobile Account Nav: 0.65%
  • Popular Pages: 0.60%
  • Header: 0.32%
  • News Lists: 0.14%

83%

That's the percent of our core space (non-header/footer) that results in less than 1% of our front page clicks.

To be fair, the top half of the right column (16.67% of the width) contains no links. But still! 83% results in 1% of clicks.

87%

That's the percent of our clicks that come from 16% of our horizontal space.

Or about 16% of our core space generates 87% of our clicks.

This includes Popular Links, Account (which includes lists) and Popular pages (which themselves are rarely used).

Further Breakdown Shows Continued Trend

All Popular Links: 58% of clicks

Of that...

  • PubMed: 62.16%
  • UpToDate: 20.99%
  • Ovid MEDLINE: 5.05%
  • Top Databases: 3.40%
  • Ebook Collections: 2.95%
  • MD Consult: 2.47%
  • Clinical Tools: 2.03%
  • Catalog: 0.71%
  • Mobile Resources: 0.24%
PubMed alone, a single text link, generated over 26% of our clicks. The display order is not indicative of actual popularity.

Summary

I can see two obvious take aways...

The link to PubMed should take up 25% of our core space.

Or, the link to PubMed should be even smaller so people click on other stuff.

Summary (more seriously)

While numbers don't lie, the do only tell you one story.

The library news promos, that take up 67% of the core space but were clicked than 1% of the time need to go away. Maybe one row and then rely on the list for more news. (although just a link to News in the Top Nav might be even more appropriate.
